Diversion

Providing one-time financial support and necessary services to aid individuals in avoiding homelessness three days before or two weeks after housing is lost.

  1. Diversion is one of the central gaps in homelessness assistance in Chapel Hill.
  2. Before Focus on Franklin, a stable funding for diversion did not exist in Chapel Hill.
  3. With the help of our partner organizations and donors, we estimate that with $6,250 we can divert 25 households, 10% of the Chapel Hill homeless population, from becoming homeless per year.

FAQ'S

  1. Q: What is the difference between diversion and prevention? A: Prevention is an overall strategy to avoid homelessness while a diversion effort is employed three days before or two weeks after experiencing homelessness.
  2. Q: How many people in Chapel Hill are homeless? A: 152 regularly but up to 250 people experience homelessness in Chapel Hill every year.
  3. Q: What is an example of a FOF diversion effort? A: An individual in Chapel Hill will become homeless if they cannot travel to live with their family in New York. FOF purchases a bus ticket for them, enabling them to reach their family and secure stable housing.
  4. Q: Are all potential homelessness cases in Chapel Hill divertable? A: No, around 10% of all cases are divertable. With FOF's funding, we hope to meet this 10% and increase the number of divertable cases.
  5. Q: How much does each diversion case cost? A: Every case is different. Efforts can range from buying a week's worth of groceries to ease tension within a household to paying an individual's utility bill to avoid eviction.
  6. Q: Which city is particularly skilled at diversion? A: Cleveland, Ohio has an exceptional diversion system, diverting around 25% of all homelessness cases. FOF aims to meet this standard.

Education Goals

We are dedicated to changing the conversation around homelessness. Our goal is to inspire action by giving the current epidemic of mass homelessness the attention it deserves. Here are a few things we think you should know:

  1. Mass homelessness is not inevitable. It has a beginning, causes, and a solution.
  2. The homeless face undue stigma. They are not a separate class; they are experiencing a housing crisis.
  3. Housing is a basic human right.
  • Chapel Hill lags behind the rest of the state in our homelessness system.
  • We are the only county in which rates of homelessness have gone up in the last eight years.
  • Our hope is that if people know to treat homelessness like the crisis it is, we can reverse this trend.
